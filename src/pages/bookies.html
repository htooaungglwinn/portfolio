Project Overview

In the year 2020 : Book pricing changed by 2.77%, which was even higher than the inflation for all items in 2020 (1.23%). This has sparked the idea of delivering a creative solution towards higher book prices and established the need of this project for avid readers.

The Problem

“Book prices set to rise as production costs soar, say UK publishers. . .”

As soon as I’ve read this catchy phrase from an article of The Guardian, I was left with tons of ideas about how I could potentially help readers with higher book prices. It kept me asking...

- Problem Statement -

"Is there a way to help avid-readers to keep up with the higher book costs?""

Paper Research

“The first three reasons for the respondents to rent the books are cost saving (30.43%) then the variety of books (22.83%), and that they have no intention to keep the book(16.30%)”

With curiosity and looking towards creative ideas to help readers with pricey books, I did some secondary research from consumer behavior towards rental book stores and got insight that the main reason of readers using book rental services is to be cost-effective.

With that in mind, I next conducted foundational research on 5 interviewees to get insight about how readers are renting books and buying books today.

Key Insight + Themes

After gathering core user data from foundational research, I then analyzed them through affinity mapping and looked for trends across different user personas. And after a week of synthesizing, there were 4 main themes of why my readers were renting books.

  • Theme 1 : Renting books to be cost-effective

    • All 5 of the interviewees have occasionally or often rented books from their close friends to be cost-effective

  • Theme 2 : The significant soaring book prices

    • Becoming difficult for students and readers with minimal earnings to buy new books

  • Theme 3 : Wasting money

    • All my interviewees have wasted money on a book they thought they would love at first but later found that the book was frustrating

  • Theme 4 : Risk when buying books

    • 4 out of 5 interviewees said that, they don’t really want to risk buying the book they don’t know or they have not read an overview or a description.

The Solution

After mapping out and synthesizing the insights derived from the secondary and foundational research, I found the main motivation of my readers renting books from friends and rental services is to be that, “Our book readers are motivated to be cost-effective on shopping recreational books” . Now, we can define our solution statement...

- Solution Statement -

“If we create a book-rent app, then readers could save money as renting books is more cost-effective than buying recreational books ”

Competative Analysis

After getting a solution statement, I started searching for similar digital solutions inside recreational books category. I then did competative anaylsis on my peer products, analyzed the opportunities and the market gap.

User Flow

Design Solutions

Upon the main insight, the major goal of the product is to be cost-saving and to be efficient

  • Access Peer To Peer

    • One tap and bring a feed of nearest peers to rent books
    • Browse through the list of peers and find the books to rent
    • Peer’s reputation plays a big role in keeping rented books in good condition

  • Engage And Send Requests

    • Pick a list of books and send request to book owner to rent them
    • Follow, chat and get in touch with your peers as in using a social media
    • Give feedbacks and rate your peers with good reputations to build a positive community

  • Rented Books Due Date Notifications

    • Get notified every upcoming rented book due date
    • A glance of request status will give enough information about the requests being due or not
    • Easily accessible due dates screen from the entry home page

Usability Study + Improvement

In the design ideating + testing phase, conducting usability study on 5 of my target users revealed that there were some design elements that didn’t convey the design principles or provide a purpose to my users. Here were some major changes after testing…

  • More accessible peer list feed

    • Initially I thought a list of nearest peers shown on a map location and a pop-up to show details of a peer would be intuitive and fun for our users to play around
    • After conducting usability study, I got feedbacks that this kind of navigation would not be always accessible and easy to navigate
    • Instead a list of peers on a simple column was easier to navigate even for non-tech savvy users

  • Conveying the Gestalt principles

    • Some design elements in the initial design of a request screen did not convey the Gestalt principles at first
    • During the usability study, most of my participants did not know what and where to press to get to the next screen
    • Applying the Gestalt principles to some design elements had a great impact on understanding the functionality of each element

  • Removing unnecessary feature UPON feedbacks

    • Something that is not useful is unlikely to be accepted by any user Interaction Design Foundation (IDF)
    • After getting feedbacks such as, “not useful” , “maybe useful but not sure to use it frequently” from 4 participants out of 5 concerned with the in-app note-taking feature, I decided to remove it from the app
    • In the end, no matter how intuitive a feature was, if users weren’t using it, it could not be a useful or a helpful one.

High-Fi mockups

After a span of 14 weeks, I achieved the final product screens, iterating and improving upon user feedbacks and insights. Even though there would be no “Final Products”, I believe that my design solutions were helpful and useful to my target users since 8 out of 9 participants could complete the app’s main user-flow in the last round of conducting usability study.

Humbly, I have come a long way with this project and of course, it has guided me towards areas that I could improve upon next.

Lesson Learned

  • If I had more time...

    • I’d love to rewind the design thinking process to do more branding and iterate the app from the business perspectives since there would be constraints and edge-cases in the real projects.

  • Insights and iterations go hand in hand

    • One important thing I have learnt was to iterate and repeat on design solutions, once there were new insights and user feedbacks plus to not hesitate and procrastinate even if it was in a high-fidelity design phase.

  • Wild ideas but are they accessible?

    • In the initial repetitions of my designs, there were some design elements and colors that were intuitive and aesthetic but not quite useful and accessible for users. After getting feedbacks from users, I dropped them off and improved my designs to be more accessible and meaningful.

  • Are you giving it all during research interviews

    • I have found that if I were always mindful about my users in the front and back of my mind during interviews, I would get better and improve getting detail insights after sitting more research interviews. Now, I can’t wait to sit a next one!

But, UX Design is a never ending story and there are still plenty of rooms for me to improve to become a designer who puts his users even in the back of his unconscious mind.
