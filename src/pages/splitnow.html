The Problem

Splitting bill among friends takes time and can often lead to stressful situations

When dining out with friends and dividing the bill, particularly when unfamiliar acquaintances are present, the procedure often becomes time-consuming and occasionally stressful. So, how can we address this challenge effectively?

Attitudinal Research

Splitting bills in real life can be time-consuming due to a LACK OF TRANSPARENCY in communication between the involved parties.

After carrying out five individual user interviews and then conducting thematic coding analysis, we have come to identify trends and themes on our user’s bill splitting experiences.

Key Insight 01: User’s bill splitting behaviors

  • Splitting equally is not always the case
  • Users want the app to accept the total and split evenly
  • And, users also expect to be able to split individually as well

Design solution: Give users options to freely customize each party split amount and roll back to splitting equally without further user manual input. Let users remove and add more parties amid the procedure

Key insight 02: The Awkwardness of verbal communication

  • Splitting the bill requires further communications among parties
  • Reminding friends to repay their share can also lead to social insecurities. 
  • Currently, there's no way to remind friends to pay back other than through verbal communication.

Design solution: Having the system take care of asking back the money from a friend will remove social insecurities and thus maintain both parties' positive relationships and there will be no awkward verbal communication.

Key insight 03: The Process of splitting bills

  • The process of splitting the bill doesn't end immediately, as some friends like to repay later
  • However, following up to payback isn’t always necessary between friends
  • Splitting bill takes time even within a group of friends and collages

Design solution: Streamline the bill splitting experience. Make a transaction first to pay the total and collect each friend's share afterwards. Keep the split record to see if all the parties have completed their parts of the bill.

User Testing + Improvements

Next the mid-fidelity variants were discussed over a cup of ☕ with my mentor to prepare prototypes for user testing. We then carried out 4 user testing sessions. The results were then analyzed and found that there were two major roadblocks in our app.

Improvement 01: Simplified Home layout and improved contrast to enhance navigation

  • In the early version of the home screen wireframe, out of 4 participants, only two passed the task in finding the splitting entry point.
  • One user took nearly 3 minutes to finally press on the right call to action.
  • Enhancing visual hierarchy and the contrast between the main and secondary ctas helped users better in finding the entry point. (Later in the second round of user testing...)

Improvement 02: A more efficient “editing” interaction flow

  • Users were confused that “edit icon” was to change the price of each friend
  • Switching the “edit icon” with a “radio” for removing friends to eliminate confusion 
  • Also removing an extra step left us with a more efficient editing flow in our app

High Fidelity Mockups

Upon reflecting user feedbacks and pain points from the user testing sessions, the wireframes are iterated and enhanced for a better usability.

We then turned the wireframes into high-fidelity interface screens, creating a design system that aligns with the brand and the tone of the product.

Second Round User Testing

With high-fidelity prototypes, we then, prepared for the second round of user testing to get feedbacks on how we have improved our app's usability.

Here are the feedbacks in conclusion after second round of user testing.

Achieved an increased 80% of task completion rate for finding entry point in the app.

Every tested user succeeded seamlessly in completing the rest of the tasks, a total of 10 tasks.

Achieved 40 Net Promoter Score(NPS) when testing the final versions of prototypes.

Reflection

  • Every design decision should have an objective

    • From this project, I've come to learn that from writing down research questions to accessing the problems with design solutions, every action we took should answer "Why are we doing this for?" and "What is it that we want to discover?" so that every design decision we made leaves an impact on tackling our target users' problems.

  • Doing it a million time on the draft table

    • A major learning for me was also that instead of getting hands on the figma, when an idea or design solution comes to my mind, writing down or sketching will do much better to later on board with the team and turn the ideas into actual design solutions.

  • User testing tackles not only usability but also accessibility

    • Testing the interfaces with the target segment will not only uncover valuable insights but also tackle the actual user needs, pain points and edge cases since we designers may never be the actual users of our product.

Moving on with my user centric design journey, I'm grateful for the opportunity to have worked on this project and look forward to applying these learnings to future endeavors.
